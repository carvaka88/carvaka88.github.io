The spread of the Persian in the subcontinent began with the conquest of Ghazni’s conquest of the Punjab. Although Persian was not the mother tongue of most of the rulers, it became the language of administration, courts, literary activity and high culture. The Indo-Persian cultural tradition began during this period.

Farsi is Arabicised form of Parsi, the Arabic language does not have the phoneme /p/. Zoroastrians in India are called Parasis. The use of the term Parsi in English dates to 1790 whereas the term Farsi dates to 1926. After the Arab conquests, the Coptic language in Egypt and Aramaic dialects in Syria went extinct and were replaced by Arabic. However Persian mainiained its status as adminstrative language but saw its syntax and grammar significantly changed especially after establishment of Sahii Islam as the state relitoin by Safavid dynasty in 1502. The old Pahlavi script was replaced by a modified form of the Arabic alphabet.

Indo-Persian and Iranian Persian

The view that Indo-Persian is inferior to Iranian Persian is a more recent and has more to do with nationalism than any inherent quality. Indians and Iranians living in India produced more important scholarly work than Iranians in Iran.

Most of the British scholarship on Persian in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was carried out by East India company bureaucrats in India. The texts maintained by the Parsi community in India were used to construct the history of ancient Iran.

During the Mughal reign, the Iranians continued to arrive at the Mughal court so foreign influence flourished. Persian being their mother tongue, they were welcomed and appointed at high posts without much competition. The Iranians were arrogant people and had a condescending attitude for the way the Indians spoke and wrote the Persian language. Shaikh Ali Hazin (d.1766) who arrived in India in the later Mughal period poured scorne on the Persian literary works of the Indians.

The linguistic imperialism created a sense of inferiority among Indians while language created a gulf between the ruling classes and the common people. They always looked up to the Iranians for approval of their language but the Iranians refused to accept it as part of their literature. The tragic result was that after the fall of the Mughal Empire, Persian literature created by Indian writers disappeared.

After the arrival of the British, English became the official language. The Indian elite learnt it and became a part of the ruling class. Those who composed English poetry and wrote short stories and novels in English perhaps suffered a fate similar to the Persian writers who wasted their creativity on foreign language and lost their work without a trace.
