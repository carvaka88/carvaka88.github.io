Modalities

The historiographic Modality

(page 5)

In British India, this modality is the most complex, pervasive, and powerful, underlying a number of the other specific modalities. … History in its broadest sense was a zone of debate over the ends and means of their rulership of India.

A second strand of the HM involved the ideological construction of the nature of Indian civilization as typefied in the major historical writings of Alexander Dow, Robert Orne, Charles Grant, Mark Wilks, James Mill, and James Todd. … beyond this they can be seen to have begun the formation of a legitimizing discourse about Britain’s civilizing mission in India.

A third strand involves histories of the British in India. .. Black Hole of Calcutta, the defeat of Tipu Sultan, or the siege of Lucknow involved the creation of emblematic heroes and villains, ..; their “history” was made concrete through the consturction of memorials and sacred spaces in India.

The Survey Modality

... the concept of the “survey” came to cover any systematic and official investigation of the natural and social features of the Indian empire.

The Enumerative Modality

It is my hypothesis that what was entailed in the construction of the census operation was the creation of the social categories by which India was ordered for administrative purposes. .. the project also objectified social, cultural, and lingustic differences among the people of India. The panoptical view that the British were constructing led to the reification of India as polity in which conflict, from the point of view of the rulers, could only be controlled by the strong hand of the British.

The Museological Modality

For many Europeans India was a vast museum, .. source of collectibles and curiosities to fill European museums, botanical gardens, zoos, and country houses.

... The power to define the nature of the past and establish priorities in the creation of the monumental record of a civilization, and to propound canons of taste, are among the most significant instrumentalities of rulership.

British Power and Indian Knowledge

H.T. Colebrooke, in a letter to his father, described the ambivalence that characterized much of the British reaction to the Indian culture:

“The further our literary inquiries are extended here, the more vast and stupendous the scene which opens to us; at the same time that the true and the false, the sublime and puerile, wisdom and absurdity, are so intermixed, that at every step, we have eo smile at folly, while we admire and acknowledge the philosophical true, though couched in obscure allegory and puerile fable”

...

Even with the undoubted good will and best intentions on part of Duncan, Hastings and others, a British metalogin of regularity, uniformity and above all fiscal responsibility could not help but participate in the erosion of what the British wanted to preserve, that is, Hindu and Muslim learning.

... there was a major epistemological gulf between two cultures as well. … What baffled the British most about Indians’ prodigious feats of memorization was that it appeared to them that the Indians did not know the meaning of what they had internalized so effectively.

What Ellis was pointing to was that the Indian more knowing and thinking was radically different from what the British assumed was the natural or normal form, and which they used as a standard by which they could adjudge Indian forms of knowledge as marred or inadequate, rather than different. Indian reasoing was based, Ellis wrote, on “the habit of their education,” which rested on the memorization of the “concentrated not diffuse knowledge,” which was easier to comprehend in verse form.

Chapter 4: Artifacts, Antiques, and Art in 19th century India

... India was seen by the Europeans not only as exotic and bizarre but as a kind of living museum of the European past.
